# Fire Without Smoke: Why the Biggest Geopolitical Crisis in Years Didn't Crash the Economy

In 2025, geopolitical risk surged to its highest sustained levels since the post-9/11 period -- driven by escalating US-Israel-Iran tensions. For months on end, the index sat deep in crisis territory.

And yet... nothing happened. No recession. No inflation spike. GDP kept growing. CPI sat at 2.65%.

How is that possible?

This is the puzzle I set out to solve in my new paper, *"Fire Without Smoke: Geopolitical Risk and the New Inflation Decoupling."*

**The key insight:** Geopolitical risk alone doesn't cause economic damage. It needs open transmission channels -- energy prices, food prices, supply chains, import costs, and inflation expectations -- to reach the real economy.

I built a new index called **GIPI** (Geopolitical Inflation Pressure Index) that measures exactly this: are the pipes through which geopolitical shocks travel to prices open or closed?

The answer in 2023-25? Completely closed.

- US shale production hit a record 13.5 million barrels/day, replacing any supply gap
- The IEA coordinated strategic reserve releases within days of each escalation
- The Fed held rates at 3.72%, keeping inflation expectations anchored

The correlation between geopolitical risk and inflation pressure over this period? Just 0.13. Compare that to 2022, when Russia's invasion blew every channel wide open and CPI hit 9.1%.

**Same fire. No smoke.**

But here's what makes this more than an academic exercise: I also built a real-time monitoring pipeline (Layer 0) that tracks these channels daily using live news data, LLM-coded event signals, and tanker freight rates. It's designed to answer one question in real time: **is the decoupling holding?**

As of late March 2026, with the Strait of Hormuz under stress, we're watching this framework get its first real out-of-sample test. The channels are starting to creak. If US production drops, reserves deplete, or expectations de-anchor -- the smoke returns.
